But why does sideways motion steal time?

Show the light‑clock animationHide animation

The formula is exact, but it doesn't explain itself. Picture a clock made of two mirrors with a pulse of light bouncing between them; each bounce is one tick. At rest, the pulse travels straight up and down. Once the clock is moving, an outside observer sees the pulse trace a diagonal — a longer path. Light's speed is the same in every frame, so a longer path means more time per tick. The moving clock runs slow, and so does every clock riding with it — including the biology of the crew.

After five seconds, the stationary clock has ticked five times; the moving one, four. That missing tick is the same deficit that puts the ship's clock behind Earth's on every trip in the simulator above.

Every number above is exact for a relativistic rocket holding constant proper acceleration, flipping at the midpoint. A real ship would still have to solve fuel, radiation, and navigation at near‑c — problems the simulator skips — but the time‑dilation arithmetic is what would actually unfold. The fiction (Project Hail Mary, Interstellar, The Forever War) gets this part right.
